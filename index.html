chatgpt is blowing up my phone
(claude code is blowing up my mind)

March 2026

I have come upon a new predicament in my software engineering journey, one characterized by constant frazzle, increasingly uncontrollable urges to continue working, and, perhaps unsurprisingly, unyielding productivity.

I am of course talking about the advances made in Claude Code and Codex models, especially after the November/December inflection point that so many have pointed out, brought on by stark model and UX improvements that have made agentic coding non-negotiable in my day to day engineering work.

In order to understand why this is so overwhelming, let me tell you a bit about myself. I am an avid texter. Probably shouldn't publicize this, but I'm a pretty good texter, and I don't really miss texts (sorry to my textees — if I haven't responded it's because I'm thinking really hard of something funny to say). In fact, when someone responds right after I send the message, I sort of get whipped up into this frenzy where we're both quipping back and forth and reacting and replying and I just lean in to the impulse to continue the conversation until one of us can't keep it up and has to leave for a meeting or to make food or any number of reasons you must physically separate yourself from the phone. Also, I have a bit of a disadvantageously short attention span / working memory, and I know this about myself, so the awareness that whatever train of thought I'm currently having is going to dissolve soon (really, is actively dissolving at that very moment) also drives me toward the immediate response + back and forth combo. It's so bad that I actually have two daily calendar events that block out times when I am allowed to respond to texts, and my work computer has the Messages app notification badge turned off at all times so I don't absentmindedly think to myself, "I'd like to make that red dot go awa— oh wow I gotta respond to all these texts!"

Enter Claude Code. Claude Code always responds, but not immediately, obviously. It's cooking. Sometimes it takes a few minutes to respond. Sometimes it's really chewing on the food for thought I have given it. What do I do in that time? Why, start another chat of course! I used to have 1, maybe 2 things I was working on at a time, but now the feature requests are keeping better pace with the speed of agentic coding, and there's so much to follow up on from the previous day, and I also would love some help drafting plans for next week, and now that I think about it, I've been meaning to build out a nice script for debugging some customer issues and actually I'm being pinged about it right now on Slack, so now is as good a time as any to write up a quick paragraph explaining the predicament to my little bot friend and letting it muse on my tasks in parallel. Oh — the first prompt just finished! Let me go see what we're working with. Oh, wow, that's a lot of text. At least it's formatted nicely, and with a helpful table comparing everything. Let's give it a think. What next… As I'm writing a considerate and helpful response pointing out pitfalls, asking for clarification, probing for references in the code, a notification sound pings. It's another text from my second Claude chat! It has no knowledge of the first Claude, and it has responded with another thoughtful output demanding my full attention. And of course, during that time, a third Claude finishes, and so on and so on.

This goes on for 9 hours a day, every day. Really more, because I'm still itching to respond into the evening, worried I'll forget the state of my conversation, or worse — have to re-explain it to a brand NEW agent that doesn't have nearly the same rapport that Claude #26 and I have established (I know I can resume the conversations, big fan of /rename for that, but sometimes between the previous night and the next morning it just isn't the same lol). I physically cannot stop. It's like having 5 group chats all blowing up over different canon-altering events at the same time. In fact, it's actually worse because you can always see the unread message count on the group chat go up and up and up and think to yourself, "I'm in a meeting/driving/out/busy/asleep, I'll just have to sit this one out," but in this case, the show can't go on without me. I have to respond!!!!!

I try to stay focused on a single thread where I can, mostly because I know I should, but I'm so much more cognizant of dead time these days, and now that there is an opportunity to unleash my very smart (though sometimes very dumb) buddy on every whim, it feels like I could always be working on something more. I used to be able to crank on some acceptable portion of a feature, commit regularly and summarize at a stopping point, write a few notes to myself for tomorrow, and tidily close out my work for the day. Now, my stopping point for the day is still probably marked by a commit, but more often than not, I'm still sort of in the middle of things with my agent. Because I'm now reliably blowing past any semblance of "acceptable portion of a feature," as soon as my guy is done I'm sort of hungry for more (see here, I'm coming back for everything, etc.). I'm so bad at getting myself to a stopping point (again, my other non-Claude friends simply stop responding to me, which … fair), and I keep thinking of more things to do, and there's no social contract stopping me from replying and spawning new threads and new threads and new threads, such that by the time the day is over, even if I try to make notes for myself, it's all so WIP-y that my work notes have become a mess and I've simply started saving my prompts / screenshots from each tab.

By now you may be thinking to yourself, "well, this is just a bad and unsustainable workflow," which, girl, same. But this experience has uncovered a new-to-me issue in somewhere between "ahhh are the jobs going away" and "is this code actually correct," as this problem is sort of orthogonal to both. AI-assisted development has grown so expansive and at such a breakneck speed, that it has created a massive divergence and sprawl in workflows, ways of thinking, and best practices. It used to feel like we were consolidating around a set of possibly-hotly-debated-but-still-tractable "best ways of writing code so you don't go insane" — git work trees, helpful commit messages, pull requests, tests, white boarding, pair programming, things like that. But now, there's infinitely many meta-options involved in how AI can integrate into each one of those things to supercharge your output and possibly also electrocute your brain in the process. Back in the day, I might meet one or two unhinged coworkers who have, like, the most f*cked git setup of all time full of thousands of lines of unpushed — nay, unstaged — code but it's kinda fine because it works for them and I just smile and nod. But now? (<- purposeful chat-ism added for effect) AI is turning me into that coworker, and I'm not sure what to do about it (edit: I do know what to do about it — sit down and figure out my workflow without the pressure of churning out features/fixes, for like, 45 minutes tops, and that would probably about do it, but now I've used that time to write this missive instead). Engineering is still thinking-intensive, but it's taxing in a very different type of way.

Again, this is not even about prompt engineering, Claude skills, or using plan mode, or saving some agents for hard-effort and some for medium-effort, or Claude via web or Claude via Slack or CLAUDE.md files or any and all AI sauce being slathered on top of the emoji-wielding HAL-9000 I now consider an indispensable part of my development workflow. It's also not about whether I think software engineering is cooked or not (discussion for another day). It's truly just about how many things I'm thinking about at the same time, which is a lot. Is my brain uniquely ill-equipped to handle this type of workload? Or are we all actually doing an okay job? What are other people doing and are you also going a bit haywire with the multitasking (which, famously, we're not supposed to do)? Let me know lol. I'm sure other folks have made this point more eloquently than I have already, but my brain is frying and I just had to tell you all about it.

Thanks for reading. This was in fact all written by hand, except for when I pasted it into Claude to generate this github page and it took it upon itself to correct my spelling of "rapport," "disadvantageously," and "unyielding." Good bot.
